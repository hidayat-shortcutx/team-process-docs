Purpose

This process exists to establish, maintain, and enforce a strong, cohesive brand identity for each of the four brands — Shortcutx, Tradishot, Solulu Beauty, and Omiee — ensuring consistency across all platforms, touchpoints, and materials, from digital to physical.

Scope

In Scope

  • Defining and documenting brand guidelines for each brand — fonts, colours, tone of voice, visual style, logo usage, and imagery direction
  • Reviewing all outgoing creative and marketing materials for brand guideline compliance before publication
  • Updating and evolving brand guidelines as each brand matures, with CEO alignment
  • Conducting periodic brand audits across all active channels and touchpoints (website, social, packaging, ads, retail)
  • Onboarding new team members and external partners on brand standards

Out of Scope

  • Hands-on execution of every individual asset — the Brand Manager oversees, reviews, and directs; production is handled by the creative team, Digital Marketing Manager, and external partners
  • Media buying and ad budget allocation — owned by the Media Manager
  • Shopify storefront technical management — owned by the Digital Marketing Manager
  • eCommerce operations (pricing, inventory, fulfilment) — owned by the eCommerce Manager and Executive

Process Flow

  1. Define or update brand guidelines for each brand (fonts, colours, tone of voice, logo usage, imagery style), aligned with the CEO.
  2. Document the guidelines in a shared, accessible brand book per brand and distribute to all relevant team members and external partners.
  3. Review all outgoing marketing and creative materials against brand guidelines before they go live — flag and correct any deviations.
  4. Conduct a quarterly brand audit across all active channels (website, social, ads, packaging, retail POSM) to identify inconsistencies.
  5. Address any deviations found in the audit — brief corrective updates to the responsible team member with a clear deadline.
  6. Onboard any new team members or external partners (agencies, freelancers) on the brand standards for all relevant brands.
  7. Evolve the guidelines over time as each brand matures — propose changes to the CEO, update the brand book, and communicate changes to the team.

Process Flow Diagram

Detailed Steps

Step 1: Define / Update Brand Guidelines▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: At brand launch, and revisited quarterly or when a significant brand evolution is needed
How:
  1. For each brand (Shortcutx, Tradishot, Solulu Beauty, Omiee), define the core brand elements: primary and secondary colour palettes, typography (fonts, weights, hierarchy), logo usage rules (clear space, minimum size, acceptable placements), tone of voice, and imagery style.
  2. Align proposed guidelines or any changes with the CEO before finalising.
  3. Ensure each brand's identity is distinct yet sits coherently under the wider company portfolio.
Output: A finalised, CEO-aligned set of brand guidelines for each brand.
Step 2: Document & Distribute Brand Book▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: After guidelines are finalised, and updated whenever guidelines change
How:
  1. Compile guidelines into a structured brand book (digital document or deck) per brand, covering all visual and verbal standards.
  2. Store in the shared Google Drive (or equivalent) so all team members and external partners can access the latest version.
  3. Notify all relevant stakeholders when a new or updated brand book is published.
Output: An accessible, up-to-date brand book per brand.
Step 3: Review Materials for Brand Compliance▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Ongoing — before any creative or marketing material goes live
How:
  1. Review submitted creative and marketing assets (ads, social posts, packaging, website banners, EDMs, POSM) against the brand guidelines for the relevant brand.
  2. Check fonts, colours, logo placement, tone of voice, imagery style, and overall visual coherence.
  3. Approve compliant materials for publication; flag non-compliant materials with specific feedback for the creator to revise.
Output: Only on-brand materials reach publication.
Step 4: Conduct Quarterly Brand Audit▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Quarterly
How:
  1. Audit all live touchpoints for each brand — website, social media profiles, active ads, packaging, retail POSM, and email templates.
  2. Identify any inconsistencies or deviations from the brand guidelines.
  3. Document findings and brief corrective updates to the responsible team member with a clear deadline.
  4. Share audit findings and corrective actions with the CEO and Head of Marketing.
Output: A quarterly brand audit report with tracked corrective actions.
Step 5: Onboard on Brand Standards▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: When a new team member joins or a new external partner (agency, freelancer, KOL) is engaged
How:
  1. Walk the new team member or partner through the brand book for each brand they will work on.
  2. Highlight the most common compliance pitfalls and the review process they will need to follow.
  3. Provide access to the brand book and any supporting assets (logo files, colour swatches, font files).
Output: New team members and partners are equipped to produce on-brand work from day one.

Exceptions and Edge Cases

ScenarioWhat to Do
A piece of creative is submitted for a brand whose guidelines are not yet finalised (e.g. Solulu Beauty or Omiee pre-launch)Review against the draft guidelines and flag that the brand book is pending finalisation — approve provisionally if aligned, but note it will need re-checking once guidelines are locked.
An external partner repeatedly deviates from brand guidelinesEscalate to the CEO and consider providing additional onboarding, closer review checkpoints, or replacing the partner.
A campaign concept requires deliberately bending a brand guideline (e.g. a special-edition colour palette)The Brand Manager approves the exception explicitly and documents it — exceptions must not set a silent precedent.
A brand audit reveals widespread off-brand content already livePrioritise corrections by visibility (highest-traffic or customer-facing channels first) and set a deadline for full remediation.

Purpose

This process exists to ensure the Brand Manager leads the overall creative and marketing direction across all four brands — driving fresh ideas, conceptualising product launches, brand activations, and campaigns, and planning and executing high-performing marketing campaigns across digital, social, and offline channels.

Scope

In Scope

  • Setting the overall creative and marketing direction for each brand, per quarter and per campaign
  • Conceptualising ideas for product launches, brand activations, and campaigns — from initial concept through to creative brief
  • Planning marketing campaigns across digital, social, and offline channels, including timelines, messaging, and channel mix
  • Briefing the creative/marketing team on campaign execution
  • Reviewing campaign output to ensure creative quality and strategic alignment before launch
  • Post-campaign review — assessing what worked, what didn't, and feeding insights into future campaigns

Out of Scope

  • Hands-on day-to-day execution of individual campaign assets — delegated to the creative team, Digital Marketing Manager, and eCommerce team
  • Media buying, budget pacing, and ad platform management — owned by the Media Manager
  • EDM scheduling and Klaviyo management — owned by the Digital Marketing Manager
  • eCommerce campaign execution (discount setup, landing pages, Shopify configuration) — owned by the eCommerce Manager/Executive

Process Flow

  1. Set the overall creative and marketing direction for the quarter, per brand, aligned with the CEO's strategic priorities.
  2. Conceptualise campaign or product launch ideas — develop the creative concept, key messaging, and the story the campaign will tell.
  3. Plan the campaign: define the timeline, channel mix (digital, social, offline), target audience, key deliverables, and success criteria.
  4. Brief the creative and marketing team on the campaign — communicate the concept, timeline, deliverables, and brand standards to follow.
  5. Review all campaign output before launch — ensure creative quality, strategic alignment, and brand compliance.
  6. If output is not up to standard, provide specific feedback and request revisions before clearing for launch.
  7. After the campaign, conduct a post-campaign review — gather performance data, assess what worked and what didn't, and document insights for future campaigns.

Process Flow Diagram

Detailed Steps

Step 1: Set the Overall Creative & Marketing Direction▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Quarterly, with mid-quarter adjustments as needed
How:
  1. Review each brand's positioning, recent performance, and market context to identify the creative themes and priorities for the quarter.
  2. Align the creative direction with the CEO's strategic priorities for each brand.
  3. Communicate the direction to all team leads so campaign-level planning can begin from a shared foundation.
Output: A clear creative direction for each brand for the quarter.
Step 2: Conceptualise Campaign / Product Launch▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Per campaign or product launch, as driven by the calendar or opportunity
How:
  1. Develop the creative concept: the central idea, visual direction, and emotional tone of the campaign or launch.
  2. Define the key messaging and the story the campaign will tell — what the audience should feel, know, and do.
  3. Consult with the CEO and Head of Marketing for input on strategic fit and ambition level.
Output: A campaign concept ready to be turned into a plan and brief.
Step 3: Plan Campaign (Timeline, Channels, Messaging)▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: After concept is finalised
How:
  1. Define the campaign timeline: key milestones, asset deadlines, and go-live date.
  2. Select the channel mix — which digital, social, and offline channels will carry the campaign, and what role each plays.
  3. Specify the target audience and any segmentation the campaign requires.
  4. List all key deliverables (e.g. hero visuals, social posts, EDMs, landing page, packaging, POSM) and assign ownership to the relevant team members.
  5. Define success criteria — what the campaign needs to achieve to be considered effective.
  6. Consult with the Digital Marketing Manager, eCommerce Manager, and Media Manager to confirm feasibility and timing.
Output: A complete campaign plan ready to brief to the team.
Step 4: Brief the Team▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Once the campaign plan is finalised
How:
  1. Communicate the campaign concept, timeline, deliverables, and brand standards to all executing team members.
  2. Ensure each team member understands their specific deliverables and deadlines.
  3. Make the brief available in a shared location (ClickUp or Google Drive) so it can be referenced throughout execution.
Output: The team is aligned and clear on what to deliver, by when.
Step 5: Review Campaign Output Before Launch▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Before any campaign material goes live
How:
  1. Review all campaign deliverables for creative quality, strategic alignment with the concept, and brand guideline compliance.
  2. Approve materials that meet the standard; flag those that don't with specific, actionable feedback for revision.
  3. Do not clear a campaign for launch until all key materials meet the required standard.
Output: Campaign materials cleared for go-live.
Step 6: Post-Campaign Review▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Within 1 week of campaign end
How:
  1. Gather performance data from the Digital Marketing Manager (EDM, social, website), eCommerce Manager (sales, conversion), and Media Manager (ad performance).
  2. Assess what worked, what didn't, and why — creative execution, channel effectiveness, audience resonance, timing.
  3. Document insights and actionable recommendations for future campaigns.
  4. Share the review summary with the CEO.
Output: A post-campaign review document with insights feeding into the next campaign cycle.

Exceptions and Edge Cases

ScenarioWhat to Do
An urgent campaign opportunity arises outside the quarterly plan (e.g. trending moment, competitor response)Fast-track the concept and brief process — compress timelines but do not skip the review step before launch.
Campaign output from the team consistently misses the briefDebrief with the team to identify the gap (brief clarity, capability, workload) and adjust the briefing or resourcing for the next campaign.
A campaign is underperforming mid-flightAssess whether a mid-campaign creative or messaging pivot is feasible; if so, brief a rapid revision; if not, document the learning for the post-campaign review.
Multiple brands have overlapping campaign timelinesPrioritise by strategic impact and stagger where possible; ensure the team's workload is manageable and flag resource conflicts to the CEO early.

Purpose

This process exists to ensure the Brand Manager oversees the production of impactful marketing assets — videos, posters, social media content, and packaging — across all four brands, ensuring every asset meets the creative vision and brand standards before it reaches the audience.

Scope

In Scope

  • Overseeing the production pipeline for all marketing assets: video content (brand films, product videos, reels), static visuals (posters, key visuals, social media graphics), and packaging design
  • Directing the creative team, content creators, videographers, and external partners on asset requirements and quality standards
  • Producing static ad creative (10 assets per funnel stage, 30 total across TOFU, MOFU, BOFU) as part of the hyper-personalised ad strategy
  • Reviewing and approving all produced assets before handoff or publication
  • Managing production timelines to ensure assets are ready ahead of campaign and launch deadlines

Out of Scope

  • Video ad creative production (EGC/UGC) for always-on ads — owned by the Digital Marketing Manager
  • EDM content creation and Klaviyo build — owned by the Digital Marketing Manager
  • Website banner and storefront updates — owned by the Digital Marketing Manager
  • Printing and physical distribution of packaging or POSM — coordinated with vendors/logistics

Process Flow

  1. Identify asset requirements from the campaign plan, product launch timeline, or ad-hoc requests from other team members.
  2. Brief the production — communicate the creative direction, brand guidelines, specifications (dimensions, formats, platform requirements), and deadline to the relevant creator (creative team, videographer, or external partner).
  3. Oversee production progress — check in at key milestones (concept/storyboard, rough cut/draft, final) to ensure the output is tracking to the brief.
  4. Review and approve completed assets — confirm creative quality, brand compliance, and technical specifications before clearing for use.
  5. If an asset does not meet the standard, provide specific feedback and request a revision with a clear deadline.
  6. Hand off approved assets to the relevant team member (Digital Marketing Manager, Media Manager, eCommerce team) or upload to the shared asset library.

Process Flow Diagram

Detailed Steps

Step 1: Identify Asset Requirements▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Ahead of each campaign or product launch, and on ad-hoc requests
How:
  1. Reference the campaign plan, product launch timeline, or incoming requests from the Digital Marketing Manager, eCommerce Manager, or Media Manager to determine what assets are needed.
  2. List the required assets: type (video, static visual, packaging), format, dimensions, platform, and deadline.
  3. For always-on ads, prepare the static ad creative batch (10 assets per funnel stage — TOFU, MOFU, BOFU — 30 total) as part of the hyper-personalised ad strategy.
Output: A clear asset requirements list with deadlines.
Step 2: Brief Production▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Once asset requirements are defined
How:
  1. Create a production brief for each asset or batch, covering: creative direction, brand guidelines reference, technical specifications (dimensions, format, resolution), platform requirements, and deadline.
  2. Assign to the appropriate creator — creative team member, content creator, videographer, or external partner.
  3. Share the brief via ClickUp or Google Drive for reference throughout production.
Output: Production briefs issued to creators with clear expectations.
Step 3: Oversee Production▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Throughout the production period
How:
  1. Check in at key milestones: concept/storyboard stage, rough cut or draft stage, and final delivery.
  2. Provide directional feedback early to avoid major rework at the final stage.
  3. Monitor progress against the deadline and flag risks early if production is falling behind.
Output: Production stays on-track and aligned with the brief.
Step 4: Review & Approve Assets▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: When final assets are submitted
How:
  1. Review each asset for creative quality, alignment with the brief and campaign concept, and brand guideline compliance.
  2. Confirm technical specifications (dimensions, format, file size, resolution) match the requirements.
  3. Approve assets that meet the standard; flag those that don't with specific, actionable feedback and a revision deadline.
Output: Approved assets ready for handoff or publication.
Step 5: Hand Off Approved Assets▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Once assets are approved
How:
  1. Upload approved assets to the shared Google Drive asset library, organised by brand, campaign, and asset type.
  2. Notify the relevant team member (Digital Marketing Manager, Media Manager, eCommerce team) that assets are ready for use.
  3. For static ad creative, hand off the batch to the Media Manager via the Google Shared Drive for platform upload.
Output: Assets delivered to the right team members, ready for deployment.

Exceptions and Edge Cases

ScenarioWhat to Do
An asset deadline is at risk because the creator is behind scheduleAssess what can be simplified or deprioritised; if critical, escalate resourcing to the CEO or bring in external support.
An external partner delivers assets that are significantly off-briefProvide a detailed revision brief; if the pattern persists, consider replacing the partner and onboarding a new one.
A last-minute asset request comes in with no lead timeAssess feasibility — if it can be done without compromising quality, prioritise it; if not, negotiate a realistic deadline with the requester.
Packaging design changes are needed after production has begunCoordinate with the vendor to assess cost and timeline impact; escalate to the CEO if the change is significant.

Purpose

This process exists to ensure the Brand Manager curates and elevates each brand's social media presence — particularly on Instagram and TikTok — delivering aesthetic, engaging, and on-brand content that builds community and drives brand affinity across Shortcutx, Tradishot, Solulu Beauty, and Omiee.

Scope

In Scope

  • Setting the visual and content direction for each brand's social media profiles (Instagram, TikTok, and any future platforms)
  • Curating the content calendar — themes, post types, visual style, and cadence per brand
  • Reviewing and approving all social content before publication for aesthetic quality and brand alignment
  • Directing the content creator and creative team on social-specific content production
  • Monitoring overall social presence quality and adjusting direction as needed

Out of Scope

  • Day-to-day social media scheduling, posting, and community management — owned by the Digital Marketing Manager
  • Paid social ad management and budget — owned by the Media Manager
  • Social media performance reporting and analytics deep-dives — owned by the Digital Marketing Manager

Process Flow

  1. Set the social content direction for each brand — define the visual aesthetic, content pillars, and tone that will drive the feed, per platform (Instagram, TikTok).
  2. Curate the monthly content calendar: plan the mix of post types (reels, carousels, stories, static posts, TikToks), themes, and cadence for each brand.
  3. Direct content production — brief the content creator and creative team on the specific content needed, including visual references, captions, and hashtag strategy.
  4. Review and approve all social content before it is scheduled or published — check aesthetic quality, brand alignment, caption copy, and hashtags.
  5. If content does not meet the visual or brand standard, provide specific feedback and request a revision before clearing for publication.
  6. Monitor the overall social presence quality on an ongoing basis — assess feed aesthetics, engagement patterns, and audience growth — and adjust the content direction as needed.

  3. Adjust the content direction, pillar weighting, or visual style based on what's working and evolving audience preferences.
Output: A continuously refined social presence that stays relevant and visually compelling.

Purpose

This process exists to ensure the Brand Manager stays ahead of industry trends, competitor activity, and emerging best practices — and translates those insights into actionable recommendations that keep all four brands relevant, differentiated, and inspiring.

Scope

In Scope

  • Monitoring competitor brands (local and international) across product, marketing, social, and retail activity
  • Tracking industry trends — product innovation, design trends, marketing tactics, content formats, platform changes
  • Translating trend and competitor insights into actionable recommendations for brand, creative, and campaign strategy
  • Presenting insights and recommendations to the CEO and team on a regular cadence
  • Introducing best practices from outside the industry that could elevate brand performance

Out of Scope

  • Pricing and promotional benchmarking — owned by the eCommerce Manager
  • Media and ad platform trend analysis — owned by the Media Manager
  • Market research and customer surveys — conducted ad-hoc or by external partners as needed

RACI Matrix

StepResponsibleAccountableConsultedInformed
1. Monitor CompetitorsBrand ManagerBrand Manager——
2. Track Industry TrendsBrand ManagerBrand Manager——
3. Translate Insights into RecommendationsBrand ManagerBrand ManagerCEOAll team leads
4. Present to CEO & TeamBrand ManagerBrand Manager—CEO, all team leads

Process Flow

  1. Continuously monitor key competitor brands — track their product launches, campaigns, social content, packaging, retail presence, and any notable marketing moves.
  2. Track broader industry trends — product innovation, design and visual trends, emerging content formats, platform updates (Instagram, TikTok), and shifts in consumer behaviour.
  3. Identify what's relevant and actionable for each of the four brands — not everything trending is worth adopting; filter for strategic fit.
  4. Translate insights into specific, actionable recommendations — e.g. a new content format to test, a visual trend to adopt, a campaign angle inspired by a competitor's approach, or a best practice from another industry.
  5. Present findings and recommendations to the CEO and relevant team members monthly, and flag urgent insights (e.g. a competitor move that requires a response) immediately.
  6. Track the adoption and impact of implemented recommendations over time.

Process Flow Diagram

Detailed Steps

Step 1: Monitor Competitor Brands▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Ongoing — dedicated review at least weekly
How:
  1. Maintain a shortlist of key competitor brands (local and international) relevant to each of the four brands.
  2. Track their product launches, campaigns, social content, packaging updates, retail presence, and any notable marketing moves.
  3. Note what they're doing well, what they're doing differently, and any gaps or opportunities they're missing.
Output: Ongoing competitor intelligence, logged for reference.
Step 2: Track Industry Trends▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Ongoing
How:
  1. Follow industry publications, social media trend reports, platform updates (Instagram, TikTok), design trend forecasts, and relevant thought leaders.
  2. Track emerging content formats, visual styles, and marketing tactics that could be relevant to the brands.
  3. Look beyond the immediate industry — identify best practices from adjacent or unrelated categories that could be adapted.
Output: A running log of relevant trends and observations.
Step 3: Translate Insights into Recommendations▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Monthly, and immediately for urgent insights
How:
  1. Filter the month's competitor and trend observations for what's relevant and actionable — not every trend warrants a response.
  2. Develop specific recommendations tied to a brand, a channel, or a campaign — e.g. "Test carousel format on Tradishot Instagram based on competitor X's strong engagement with this format."
  3. For urgent insights (e.g. a direct competitor launching a similar product or campaign), flag and recommend a response immediately, without waiting for the monthly cadence.
Output: Actionable recommendations ready for CEO and team discussion.
Step 4: Present to CEO & Team▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Monthly, plus urgent ad-hoc as needed
How:
  1. Compile the month's key insights and recommendations into a concise summary — focus on what's actionable, not an exhaustive list of everything observed.
  2. Present to the CEO and relevant team leads; discuss which recommendations to adopt, test, or deprioritise.
  3. Track adopted recommendations and their impact over subsequent months.
Output: Monthly insights shared, with agreed-upon actions tracked.

Exceptions and Edge Cases

ScenarioWhat to Do

Purpose

This process exists to ensure the Brand Manager leads, mentors, and inspires the creative and marketing team — maintaining smooth workflows, timely delivery of all outputs, and consistently high-quality work across all four brands.

Scope

In Scope

  • Leading and mentoring the creative and marketing team — content creators, designers, videographers, and coordinators working across the four brands
  • Managing team workflows — ensuring work is prioritised, deadlines are met, and bottlenecks are resolved quickly
  • Conducting regular team check-ins and 1-on-1s to maintain alignment, morale, and development
  • Setting quality standards and providing constructive feedback to elevate the team's output over time
  • Identifying skill gaps and recommending development or hiring to the CEO

Out of Scope

  • HR administration (contracts, payroll, leave management) — handled by operations/HR
  • Performance reviews and formal appraisals — conducted in collaboration with the CEO per company policy
  • Hiring final decisions — the Brand Manager recommends; the CEO approves

Process Flow

  1. Set clear quality standards and expectations for the creative/marketing team — what "good" looks like, timelines to adhere to, and the review process to follow.
  2. Manage the team's workflow on an ongoing basis — prioritise tasks, assign ownership, balance workload across brands, and unblock bottlenecks quickly.
  3. Conduct regular team check-ins (weekly) and 1-on-1s (fortnightly or monthly) to maintain alignment, address issues early, and keep morale high.
  4. Provide constructive, specific feedback on work output — not just what needs fixing, but why and how to improve, to build the team's capability over time.
  5. Identify skill gaps or resourcing constraints that are impacting output quality or timelines; recommend development (training, mentoring) or hiring to the CEO.

Process Flow Diagram

Detailed Steps

Step 1: Set Quality Standards & Expectations▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: At onboarding and revisited quarterly
How:
  1. Define what "good" looks like for each type of output the team produces — design assets, video content, social posts, packaging — with visual examples where possible.
  2. Set clear expectations on timelines, the review/approval process, and the level of polish expected before submission.
  3. Communicate these standards to the team and make them accessible for ongoing reference.
Output: The team has a shared, clear understanding of what's expected.
Step 2: Manage Workflow & Priorities▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Ongoing — daily attention
How:
  1. Maintain visibility over all active tasks across brands — use ClickUp (or the team's task management tool) to track progress and deadlines.
  2. Prioritise tasks when competing deadlines arise, balancing urgency and strategic importance across the four brands.
  3. Identify and resolve bottlenecks quickly — whether they are resource constraints, approval delays, or unclear briefs.
Output: A well-prioritised, unblocked workflow delivering on time.
Step 3: Conduct Team Check-ins & 1-on-1s▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Weekly (team) and fortnightly or monthly (1-on-1s)
How:
  1. Run a weekly team stand-up or check-in to review the week's priorities, surface blockers, and ensure alignment across brands.
  2. Conduct fortnightly or monthly 1-on-1s with each direct report to discuss workload, development, concerns, and feedback.
  3. Use these touchpoints to maintain morale, catch issues early, and reinforce the team's connection to the brand vision.
Output: The team stays aligned, heard, and motivated.
Step 4: Mentor & Develop Team Members▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Ongoing, integrated into daily work and 1-on-1s
How:
  1. Provide constructive, specific feedback on work output — explain not just what to fix, but why and how to improve.
  2. Identify each team member's strengths and growth areas; tailor coaching accordingly.
  3. Create opportunities for team members to stretch — e.g. leading a small campaign, presenting an idea, or owning a new content format.
Output: A team that improves steadily and feels invested in.
Step 5: Identify & Address Gaps▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Quarterly review, or sooner if a gap is impacting output
How:
  1. Assess the team's skill set against the demands of all four brands — where are the strengths, and where are the gaps (e.g. video editing, motion graphics, packaging design)?
  2. Assess resourcing — is the team's capacity sufficient to deliver the required output across brands without consistent overload?
  3. Recommend to the CEO: training or upskilling for existing team members, or a hiring recommendation if the gap is structural.
Output: Gaps identified with a clear recommendation for how to close them.

Purpose

This process exists to ensure all business units and sub-brands are aligned under one cohesive brand vision, and that the Brand Manager collaborates closely with the CEO — sharing insights, creative strategies, and recommendations that drive brand growth across Shortcutx, Tradishot, Solulu Beauty, and Omiee.

Scope

In Scope

  • Aligning all business units — eCommerce, digital marketing, creative, retail — under a unified brand vision per brand and across the portfolio
  • Regular strategic check-ins with the CEO to share creative insights, campaign strategies, brand health updates, and growth recommendations
  • Proposing brand-level strategic initiatives — new brand directions, repositioning, sub-brand launches, or portfolio-level branding decisions
  • Ensuring cross-functional alignment on brand priorities — all teams pulling in the same direction
  • Representing the brand perspective in business-level decisions (e.g. new product categories, market entry, partnerships)

Out of Scope

  • Financial planning and P&L management — owned by the CEO / finance
  • Operational decisions (logistics, fulfilment, supply chain) — owned by operations
  • Final strategic decisions — the Brand Manager recommends; the CEO decides

Process Flow

  1. Define or refresh the overarching brand vision for the portfolio — what each brand stands for, how they relate to each other, and what the portfolio collectively represents — aligned with the CEO.
  2. Communicate the brand vision to all business units and ensure every team understands how their work connects to it.
  3. Conduct regular strategic check-ins with the CEO (fortnightly or as agreed) — share creative insights, campaign results, brand health observations, competitor intelligence, and growth recommendations.
  4. Propose brand-level strategic initiatives when opportunities or needs arise — new brand directions, positioning shifts, sub-brand launches, or partnerships.
  5. Ensure cross-functional alignment by proactively checking that eCommerce, digital marketing, creative, and retail activities are consistent with the brand vision — flag and correct misalignment early.

Process Flow Diagram

Detailed Steps

Step 1: Define / Refresh Brand Vision▶
Who: Brand Manager (with CEO)
When: Annually, or when a significant strategic shift occurs
How:
  1. Articulate what each brand stands for — its positioning, personality, audience, and promise — and how the brands relate to each other within the portfolio.
  2. Align the vision with the CEO's strategic direction for the company.
  3. Document the vision in a format that can be shared with all teams (deck, one-pager, or brand book preamble).
Output: A clear, CEO-aligned brand vision for the portfolio.
Step 2: Communicate to All Business Units▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: After the vision is defined, and reinforced quarterly
How:
  1. Present the brand vision to all team leads — eCommerce, digital marketing, creative, retail — and explain how their function contributes to it.
  2. Make the vision accessible and easy to reference (shared document, team channel pin, or physical display).
  3. Reinforce the vision in team check-ins and briefs — keep it alive, not just a one-time presentation.
Output: All teams understand and can connect their work to the brand vision.
Step 3: CEO Strategic Check-ins▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Fortnightly (or as agreed with the CEO)
How:
  1. Prepare a concise update covering: creative highlights and campaign results, brand health observations (consistency, perception, social sentiment), competitor and trend insights, and upcoming priorities.
  2. Share specific recommendations for brand growth — new initiatives, adjustments, or opportunities worth exploring.
  3. Discuss and align on any strategic decisions that need CEO input or approval.
Output: CEO is informed and aligned; strategic decisions are made together.
Step 4: Propose Strategic Initiatives▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: As opportunities or needs arise
How:
  1. Identify brand-level opportunities — a repositioning, a new sub-brand direction, a strategic partnership, a market entry, or a portfolio-level branding decision.
  2. Develop a proposal: the opportunity, the rationale, the expected impact, and the resources required.
  3. Present to the CEO for discussion and decision.
Output: A well-reasoned strategic proposal for CEO consideration.
Step 5: Ensure Cross-Functional Brand Alignment▶
Who: Brand Manager
When: Ongoing
How:
  1. Proactively review the output and plans of each business unit (eCommerce, digital marketing, creative, retail) for consistency with the brand vision.
  2. Flag and correct any misalignment early — before it reaches the customer.
  3. Facilitate cross-team coordination when a campaign or initiative touches multiple business units, ensuring everyone is working toward the same brand outcome.
Output: All teams are consistently pulling in the same brand direction.
